Itin Bank Account Opening: What Is an ITIN and Why Do You Need One for Banking?

An Individual Taxpayer Identification Number, or ITIN, is a tax processing number from the Internal Revenue Service (IRS). The IRS gives these nine-digit numbers to people who can not get a Social Security number (SSN) but must follow U.S. tax laws. While the IRS makes ITINs for tax use, many banks and fintech firms now use them to help more people get bank accounts.

For many Filipino immigrants and freelancers, getting an ITIN is a key step toward life in the United States. It lets you join the local economy even if you are still waiting for other papers. Having this ID helps you start a money trail and manage your cash safely.

ITIN versus SSN

The main difference between an ITIN and an SSN is who can get them. An SSN is for U.S. citizens and some residents with work permits. An ITIN is for those who do not have an SSN but must file a U.S. tax return. Both numbers have nine digits and look much the same, but an ITIN always starts with the number nine. An ITIN does not change your legal status or give you the right to work in the U.S.

Banks use these numbers to check who you are. Under federal laws like the USA PATRIOT Act, firms must check the identity of anyone opening an account. Since an ITIN is a government ID, many banks take it as a valid form of taxpayer identification. This makes ITIN bank account opening possible for many new arrivals.

Why banks accept ITINs

Banks want to help as many people as they can while staying safe and legal. By taking ITINs, they can help people who earn money and pay taxes but lack an SSN. This includes many OFWs and workers who are new to the country. It helps the bank follow "Know Your Customer" rules while giving you a safe place to keep your money.

Using an ITIN to open an account also helps you start your money life in the U.S. It lets you pay bills, get direct deposits, and save for the future. Proving your home address

The bank needs to know where you live now. They use this to send you mail and keep your account secure. You usually need a bill or a letter that shows your US address. Some banks may also ask for your home address in the Philippines. This is common if you are an Overseas Filipino Worker (OFW). It helps the bank see your ties to your home country.

  1. Valid foreign passport. This is your main photo ID. It must be current and not past its end date. Banks trust this paper the most to check your name and birth date.
  2. Second form of ID. You will need a second card with your name on it. This can be a foreign driver's license, a work visa, or a consulate ID card. Many Filipinos use their consulate ID for this step.
  3. ITIN letter. The IRS sends you a CP 565 letter when you get your number. You must show the real letter to the bank. You can find more facts on how to get this on the IRS website for tax numbers.
  4. Proof of US address. Bring a recent paper that shows where you live in the US. A utility bill, a lease, or a cell phone bill usually works. It should be less than three months old to be valid.
  5. Proof of foreign address. Some banks want to see where you live in your home country. A bank bill or a letter from the Philippines can meet this need. This helps the bank confirm your global status.
  6. Your opening deposit. Most banks need you to put money into the account to start. This is often between $25 and $100. You can use cash or a check for this first payment.

Most banks will ask for the real copies of these papers. They usually do not accept clear scans or photos. If you are applying in person, bring your real passport and letter. If you apply online, you might need to upload high-quality scans of each page. Checking these items off your list will help you get your account without any delay.

Step-by-Step: How to Open Your US Bank Account with an ITIN

Opening a bank account in the U.S. is a big goal for many Filipinos. If you do not have a Social Security Number, you can use an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) instead. This number lets you pay taxes and open accounts at many big banks.

While the process has several steps, it is now easier for immigrants to do than ever before. You can build your credit and save your money safely. By following a clear path, you can set up your money life in a new country with ease.

Getting Your Tax ID from the IRS

The first step is to get your ITIN from the IRS. You will need to fill out Form W-7 and show who you are with your ID. Many people do this when they file their first tax return. You can find all the details on Form W-7 on the main IRS site. Once you have this number, you can look for a bank that fits your needs and your life.

There was a helpful change in federal law in June 2025. The government now lets banks use a new way to help people who are waiting on their IDs. Under these FDIC rules, banks can let you open an account while they wait for your ID. This change helps you start your money life in the U.S. without long waits. You can read more about this in our guide to opening a U.S. bank account.

Picking the Right Bank for You

After you have your papers ready, you must choose a bank. Not every bank accepts an ITIN, so it is smart to call first. Ask the bank if they allow ITIN bank account opening for people who are not citizens. Most large banks will have a way to do this, but small local banks might not. Once you know they accept your ID, you can move forward with the steps below to get your account set up.

  1. Apply for your ITIN. Fill out IRS Form W-7 and send it with your passport to the IRS to get your tax ID number.
  2. Gather your ID papers. Collect your passport from the Philippines, a second form of ID like a national ID card, and a recent bill to prove your home address.
  3. Check bank rules. Call or visit the websites of local banks to find out if they accept an ITIN for new account forms.
  4. Submit your application. Visit a bank branch in person or use their website to give them your details and papers.
  5. Deposit your first funds. Add money to your new account through a check, cash, or a wire transfer to finish the setup and make it active.
