Money in America: A Starter Guide for New Filipino Nurses

You spent years becoming an excellent nurse. What nobody taught you is how money works in America — and it works differently from home. The US runs on credit, on automatic systems, and on small charges that add up unless you know to avoid them. None of it is hard once someone draws you the map. That's what this is. Each topic gets its own deeper guide later; today is the overview.

How you get paid

Most facilities pay every two weeks. The first surprise for almost every kabayan: your take-home is smaller than your salary divided up. What you earn is gross pay; what actually lands in your account is net pay. In between, the system automatically holds back taxes and contributions — much like SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG did back home, just under different names.

As a rough benchmark, expect roughly 20–30% of your gross pay to go to taxes and withholding, depending on your state. That includes about 7.65% for Social Security and Medicare (the US version of your old contributions), plus federal income tax and, in some states, state income tax.

When you start, you'll fill out a W-4 form that sets how much tax is withheld — fill it out carefully. Working nights or overtime can boost your pay nicely. We break down the full paystub, line by line, in a separate guide.

The importance of credit

If you remember one thing, make it this. In America your credit score (a number, usually 300–850) is your financial reputation. It quietly decides whether you can rent an apartment, finance a car, get a phone plan, or qualify for the best cards.

The twist for new arrivals: you don't have a bad score — you have none yet. Your score is built from what companies — credit card issuers, banks, and other lenders — report about you to the credit bureaus each month, so until one of them is reporting your activity, you stay invisible to the system. The fix is to build it on purpose, early:

  • Apply for a credit card and start spending on it.
  • Pay every bill on time.
  • Keep balances low.

Do this steadily and within a year you'll have a real score that lowers your costs on almost everything. Several guides in this series go deep here.

Taxes: pay now, settle later

Tax comes out of each paycheck automatically. After the year ends, you file a return (deadline is usually mid-April) that reconciles what you owe with what was already taken — money back if too much came out, a little owed if too little did. You'll get a W-2 form by the end of January with the totals. First-time filing is very manageable, and we'll walk you through it in a future blog post. 

Receiving money, sending money home, and your dollar buffer

In your first weeks you'll need simple ways to receive your pay, spend day to day, save, and send padala home. Two costs are worth knowing up front:

  • Sending money home has a hidden cost. The true price of a remittance is the fee plus the exchange-rate markup — a "zero fee" transfer can still shortchange your family through a worse rate. Always compare how many pesos actually arrive, not the advertised fee.
  • Moving pesos into dollars can quietly cost you, too. Many new nurses don't realize how much is lost converting cash at airport counters or through poor exchange rates. It's worth holding some of your money in dollars, converted at a fair rate, so you land with a ready dollar buffer instead of exchanging at the worst possible moment.
Quick rule of thumb

Whether you're sending pesos home or converting your savings into dollars, judge every transfer by what actually arrives — not the headline fee.
