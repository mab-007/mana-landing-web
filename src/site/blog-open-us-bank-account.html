How to Open Your First US Bank Account (Even With No Credit History)

One of the first questions every new arrival asks is, "Will they even let me open an account if I have no credit?" Here's the good news: opening a bank account and having credit are two completely different things. You do not need a credit history to open a checking or savings account in the US. What you need is the right set of documents — and a little know-how about how the system works.

Why credit doesn't matter here

A credit score is your borrowing reputation — it tells lenders how likely you are to pay back money you've been lent. A bank account is the opposite: it holds your own money. Because the bank isn't lending you anything, it doesn't check your credit score to let you open a standard checking or savings account.

What banks do check is a separate report called ChexSystems — a record of how you've handled past bank accounts (bounced checks, unpaid overdrafts, accounts closed for fraud). As a newcomer you simply have no ChexSystems history, which is perfectly fine. A blank record is not a bad record.

What you'll actually need

Requirements vary slightly by bank, but almost every one will ask for some combination of the following. Bring originals where you can:

  • A government photo ID. Your passport is the gold standard for new arrivals. Many banks will also want a second ID, such as your visa, a foreign driver's license, or a consular ID.
  • A Social Security Number (SSN) or ITIN. If you're authorized to work, you'll have an SSN. If you don't qualify for one, many banks accept an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) — and some accept a passport with no SSN at all. Ask before you assume you're stuck.
  • Proof of US address. A lease, a utility bill, or an official letter mailed to where you live. If you've just landed and don't have one yet, ask whether they accept your employer's letter or your school's enrollment document.
  • An opening deposit. Often as little as $25 — and sometimes $0. Have a way to fund it on day one.
Good to know

No SSN yet? You are not locked out. Look specifically for banks and fintechs that advertise "open an account with a passport" or "ITIN accepted." They exist precisely for people in your situation.

The steps, start to finish

  • 1. Gather your documents from the list above so you're not turned away for a missing paper.
  • 2. Choose where to open. Compare monthly fees, minimum balances, ATM access, and — importantly — whether they accept newcomers without an SSN.
  • 3. Apply. Online takes minutes; in a branch, a banker walks you through it. Either way you'll confirm your identity and address.
  • 4. Fund it with your opening deposit, then set up your debit card and online banking.

That's it. Most people walk out — or click "submit" — with a working account the same day.

An account alone won't build credit

Here's the part newcomers miss: opening a checking account does not build your credit score. Your everyday spending and balances aren't reported to the credit bureaus. To actually start building credit, you'll want a tool that does report — most commonly a secured or starter credit card used responsibly and paid on time. Think of your bank account as the foundation, and credit as the next floor you build on top of it.
